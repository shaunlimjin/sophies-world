🤯 Weird But True

Facts So Wild You'll Tell Everyone at Lunch

Octopuses Have THREE Hearts 🐙 An octopus doesn't just have one heart — it has three! Two pump blood to its gills, and one pumps blood to the rest of its body. Even wilder: its blood is blue, not red, because it uses copper instead of iron to carry oxygen. When an octopus swims, one of those hearts actually stops beating — which is why octopuses prefer crawling to swimming!
Honey Found in Pyramids Was Still Edible 🍯 Archaeologists digging inside ancient Egyptian pyramids discovered pots of honey that were over 3,000 years old — and they were still perfectly edible! Honey never spoils because it's so low in moisture and so high in natural sugar that bacteria simply cannot survive in it. Bees basically invented the world's best natural preservative.
A Snail Can Sleep for Three Years 🐌 If the weather gets too dry or cold, a garden snail can seal itself inside its shell and go into a super-deep sleep called estivation. Scientists have observed snails sleeping for up to three years waiting for better conditions. Once the rain returns, they wake up and carry on like nothing happened!

Big Stories From Around the Globe

Two important things happening in the world right now — explained just for you.

Why Some Prices at the Store Are Going Up 🛒

You may have noticed that some things at the grocery store or shoe store cost more than they used to. One big reason is tariffs — fees that countries charge when they buy goods from each other. The United States recently added new tariffs on products coming from many countries, which means things like clothing, electronics, and certain foods now cost more to bring into the country.

These higher costs get passed along to families at checkout. Economists — the people who study how money and trade work — say tariff changes can ripple through whole economies, affecting everything from the price of a T-shirt to the cost of building a house. Countries are now in conversations about how to make trade fairer for everyone.

Imagine your school has a rule: every time two students trade snacks at lunch, they each have to give the teacher 5 extra crackers as a fee. Suddenly trading feels expensive, and kids trade less. That's exactly what tariffs do — they add an extra cost to trading between countries.

Solar Power Is Taking Over the World — In a Great Way! ☀️

Here's some exciting news: solar panels have gotten about 90% cheaper over the last decade. That means energy from the sun is now affordable for more families, schools, and even whole countries than ever before. Many places in Africa, Asia, and South America that never had reliable electricity are skipping old-fashioned power plants entirely and going straight to solar.

California is one of the sunniest and most solar-powered states in the USA. On some days, California produces more solar electricity than it can use and actually shares it with neighboring states! Scientists say that solar power growing this fast is one of the most hopeful signs that we can slow down climate change.

Imagine if a supercomputer that used to cost millions of dollars suddenly became as cheap as a bag of chips. That is how dramatically solar panel prices have dropped — technology that was once only for rich countries is now reaching everyone.

🇸🇬 Singapore Spotlight

Little Red Dot, Big Surprises

Singapore Has the World's First Nighttime Zoo 🦁🌙

Singapore is home to the world's very first zoo designed just for after dark. The Night Safari opened in 1994 and lets visitors explore a jungle at night to see animals that naturally come alive when the sun goes down — like clouded leopards, fishing cats, giant flying squirrels, and striped hyenas. Special soft orange lighting makes it feel like moonlight without disturbing the animals.

Over 1,000 animals from around 100 species live there. Tram rides wind through different forest zones representing Asia, Africa, and South America — all within tiny Singapore! It has won awards for being one of the best wildlife attractions in the world and gets millions of visitors every year.

Singapore Turns Seawater Into Drinking Water 💧

Singapore is a tiny island with almost no natural freshwater — no big rivers, no deep lakes. So how does everyone have clean water? Engineers built special factories called desalination plants that remove salt from seawater to make it safe to drink. Singapore also recycles used water through advanced purification systems to create something called NEWater, which is so clean it meets drinking-water standards.

Today, Singapore produces nearly half of its own water through these methods and is considered a world leader in water technology. Countries from the Middle East to Southeast Asia have sent teams to Singapore to learn how they do it. For such a tiny place, that is one seriously impressive superpower!

🇺🇸 USA Corner

What's Happening in Our American Backyard

NASA Is Getting Ready to Return to the Moon 🚀🌕

For the first time since 1972 — over 50 years ago — NASA is preparing to send astronauts back to the Moon as part of the Artemis program. The mission aims to land the first woman and first person of color on the lunar surface. California plays a giant role: NASA's Jet Propulsion Laboratory in Pasadena designs the spacecraft and rovers that explore space, and many of the engineers who build rockets work right here in the Golden State.

The plan is not just to visit and leave. NASA wants to build a base camp on and around the Moon so astronauts can stay longer — and eventually use it as a launch point to reach Mars. Students in Fremont who love science and math today could be part of those missions in the future!

⭐ Interest Corner

Flip, Twist, Fly — Your Gymnastics World

Simone Biles Has Skills Named After Her 🏅

Simone Biles is widely considered the greatest gymnast in history — and she has the medals to prove it. She has won more World Championship medals than any other gymnast ever, plus seven Olympic medals. But here is what makes her truly legendary: she has invented skills so difficult that gymnastics judges officially named them after her. Moves like the Biles, the Biles II, and others now carry her name in the official rulebook forever.

Simone also showed incredible courage at the 2020 Tokyo Olympics when she stepped back from some events to protect her mental health — and the world cheered for her bravery. She came back and won gold at the 2024 Paris Olympics, proving that true strength means knowing when to rest and when to fly. She is a role model on and off the mat.

Try This: The Secret to a Straight Cartwheel ⭐

Cartwheels are one of the very first skills gymnasts master — and yours can get better with one simple trick! Many gymnastics coaches place a strip of tape on the floor and ask students to do their cartwheel directly along the line, with hands and feet all landing on that same straight path. This one change makes a huge difference in how clean and powerful the cartwheel looks.

Start in a lunge with your right foot forward and arms up. Look at a spot on the floor ahead of you, place your right hand down on the line, then your left hand, kick your legs up and over, and land left foot first — all on the tape. Keep your arms straight and really reach! Practice slowly first, then build speed as it gets easier.

💰 Money Moves

Smart Money Skills for Future Entrepreneurs

The Magic of Compound Interest 💸✨

Here is one of the most powerful money secrets in the world: when you save money in a bank, the bank pays you a small reward called interest. But here is where the magic happens — you also earn interest on your interest. This snowball effect is called compound interest, and over many years it can turn a small amount into a much larger one without you doing anything extra.

If you saved just $10 a month starting at age 10, by the time you are 65 you could have tens of thousands of dollars — from only about $6,600 in original savings! The math is on your side the earlier you start. Albert Einstein reportedly called compound interest the eighth wonder of the world, and once you see it in action, it is hard to argue with him.

Money Tip: Even saving $1 or $2 a week adds up faster than you think. The trick is to start early and stay consistent — time does the hard work for you!

Meet Mikaila Ulmer: Stung by a Bee, Built a Business 🐝🍋

When Mikaila Ulmer was just 4 years old, she got stung by a bee — twice! Instead of being scared of bees forever, she got curious. She learned how important bees are for growing our food and started making lemonade sweetened with honey using her great-grandmother's old recipe. She called it Me and the Bees Lemonade.

By age 11, her lemonade was on shelves at Whole Foods stores. By 14, she appeared on the TV show Shark Tank and walked away with a $60,000 investment. Today her lemonade is sold in hundreds of stores across the USA, and she donates part of every sale to honeybee protection programs. Her story proves that curiosity plus kindness equals a seriously great business idea.

🧠 Sophie's Challenge

Can You Crack This Week's Puzzle?

This week in World Watch, we learned about tariffs — the extra fees that countries add to goods coming in from other places. When tariffs go up, stores often have to charge more for things like shoes, clothes, and electronics. Let's use math to see exactly how much extra that can cost a family!

A pair of sneakers normally costs $50. Because of new tariffs, the store raises the price by 20%. What is the new price of the sneakers? If a family needs to buy 3 pairs of sneakers — one for each kid — how much extra money does the family spend in total because of the tariff, compared to the original price?
⭐ Bonus: If the tariff was 35% instead of 20%, what would one pair of sneakers cost? Round to the nearest dollar.
To find 20% of $50, multiply 50 by 0.20 — or think of it as dividing 50 by 5. Add that amount to $50 to get the new price. Then multiply the extra cost by 3 to find how much more the whole family pays.
