🤯 Weird But True

Three Brain-Bending Facts

Buckle up — these are 100% real and 100% weird.

Geckos lick their own eyeballs 👅 Geckos don't have eyelids, so they can't blink like you and me. To keep their eyes clean and wet, they use their long, stretchy tongue as a built-in windshield wiper. Imagine licking your own eyeball — gross, but genius!
Some animals sleep almost ALL day 😴 You might sleep about 8 hours a night, but koalas snooze up to 22 hours every single day. Their leafy diet doesn't give them much energy, so napping is basically their full-time job.
There's no "space goo" between the stars 🌌 Long ago, scientists believed space was filled with an invisible substance called "ether." In 1887, two American physicists ran a super-precise experiment to find it… and detected absolutely nothing. That "oops!" moment helped pave the way for Einstein's wild ideas about light and time.

🌍 World Watch

What's Happening on Planet Earth

Two big stories shaping the world this week — explained in human (and 4th-grader) language.

World leaders meet in Colombia to talk about quitting fossil fuels

On April 24, governments, scientists, and Indigenous leaders gathered in Colombia for a high-stakes summit. Their goal: figure out how to slowly stop using fossil fuels like oil, coal, and gas — the stuff that powers most of our cars, planes, and factories.

Burning fossil fuels heats up the planet, so leaders are racing to switch to cleaner energy like solar and wind. The tricky part? Fossil fuels are tangled up with jobs, money, and how people get around every day, so the change has to be careful.

Imagine your whole school runs on one giant chocolate-cake oven. The cake's tasty, but it's also making the gym too hot. Switching to a brand new oven that uses sunshine sounds great — but you have to make sure the cafeteria still works while you swap them out!

Earth Day 2026: worry, hope, and a planet that needs us

April 22 was Earth Day, and NewsForKids put out a big report on how the planet is doing. The good news: more countries are using clean energy and protecting wildlife. The hard news: wars in places like Ukraine, Sudan, and the Middle East are also hurting the environment, leaving behind pollution and destroyed land.

Scientists say small actions still matter — saving water, eating less food waste, and learning about climate are all helpful. The biggest changes come when many people (and governments) work together.

Earth is like a giant shared classroom. If one kid spills paint, it's no big deal. But if everyone spills, the floor turns into a swamp — and it takes the whole class to clean it up.

🇸🇬 Singapore Spotlight

From Tiny Islands to Michelin Plates

Singapore packs a LOT of cool into a small space. Here are two reasons why.

Singapore is one main island… plus 62 little ones! 🏝️

When people say "Singapore," they usually picture the big main island, called Pulau Ujong, which stretches about 30 miles across. But surrounding it are 62 smaller islands — some are nature reserves, some have beach resorts, and a few are completely empty.

Tucked between Malaysia (to the north) and Indonesia (to the south), Singapore is so small you could drive from one end to the other in less than an hour. Yet it's home to over 5 million people and one of the busiest ports in the world.

The world's cheapest Michelin-starred meal is… chicken rice 🍗🍚

Singapore's hawker centres are open-air food courts where chefs cook up amazing dishes for just a few dollars. In 2016, a hawker stall called Hong Kong Soya Sauce Chicken Rice and Noodle became one of the first street food spots in the world to earn a Michelin star — the same fancy award given to top restaurants.

Even cooler: that chicken rice plate cost only a couple of bucks, making it the world's cheapest Michelin-starred meal. In 2018, UNESCO even added Singapore's hawker culture to its list of important world traditions.

🇺🇸 USA Corner

California Tries to Make the Internet Safer for Kids

Big tech debates are heating up right here in your home state.

California pushes to ban social media for kids under 16

California lawmakers are moving forward with a bill that could stop kids under 16 from making social media accounts in the country's most populous state. They say apps are designed to be "addictive" — pulling kids in for hours and hurting sleep, focus, and mood.

If it passes, it would be one of the strictest rules in the country. Some families cheer the idea; others worry it goes too far. The conversation will keep going as the bill moves through the state legislature.

New rules to keep AI chatbots safer for kids

On April 22, California committees advanced two bills that would create the country's strongest protections around AI chatbots and kids. Lawmakers worry that some chatbots can give kids unsafe advice or pretend to be a human friend.

The new rules would require companies to be clearer when kids are talking to a bot (not a person) and add safety guardrails. California often sets the pace for the rest of the U.S. on tech laws — so other states are watching closely.

⭐ Interest Corner

Big Routines, Big Brains

Gymnasts aren't just strong — they're scholars too.

Clemson's Brie Clark: "Make little Brie proud" 🐅

Brie Clark just made history at Clemson University. She became the very first Clemson gymnast ever to qualify for the NCAA Championships, the program's first All-American, and part of its first ACC title-winning team.

As she gets ready for the final routine of her college career, Brie says her motivation is simple: make the little kid version of herself — the one who first fell in love with gymnastics — really proud. That's a powerful goal for anyone who loves a sport.

California gymnasts star on the 2026 ACC Academic Team 📚

The Atlantic Coast Conference just named its 2026 Gymnastics All-ACC Academic Team — and the California Golden Bears showed up in a big way. To make the list, athletes need at least a 3.20 GPA AND must compete in half of their team's meets.

Cal gymnasts on the list are studying everything from Environmental Engineering to Molecular & Cell Biology to Psychology. Translation: you can flip on the beam AND ace your bio test. Brains and balance — that's the gymnast superpower.

💰 Money Moves

Save Smart, Earn Smarter

Build a saving habit and meet kids who turned ideas into real money.

These teens earned about $10,000 — by thinking like entrepreneurs

A mom in Business Insider shared how she's been teaching her kids to think like entrepreneurs ever since they were little. She didn't just hand them money — she helped them spot small problems people would pay to solve, then taught them how to budget and save what they earned.

By their teenage years, her kids had earned about $10,000 between them. The big secret wasn't a magic business idea. It was the habit of asking "How can I help someone, and how can I save what I earn?"

🪙 Try the Match Game: ask a parent to match every $5 you save (up to a limit). It instantly turns saving into a fun challenge — and your money grows twice as fast.

🧠 Sophie's Challenge

The Colombia Climate Summit Math Mission

This week's World Watch told us about world leaders meeting in Colombia to plan how to use less fossil fuel. Let's turn that into a math mission!

Sophieville uses 200 units of energy every day. Right now, 3/4 of that energy comes from fossil fuels (oil, coal, gas), and the rest is clean energy (solar and wind). How many units come from fossil fuels, and how many come from clean energy?
⭐ Bonus: Sophieville's mayor wants to cut fossil fuel use IN HALF and replace it with clean energy. After the change, how many units of clean energy will Sophieville use each day?
To find 3/4 of 200, first split 200 into 4 equal groups (200 ÷ 4 = 50). Then count 3 of those groups. The leftover group is the clean energy.
